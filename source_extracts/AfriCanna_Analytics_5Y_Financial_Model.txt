-- Sheet: Cover --
AfriCanna Analytics — Cannabis Testing Lab Financial Model
Investor-ready five-year model for South African cannabis analytical testing laboratory
Workbook purpose
This workbook models a phased cannabis testing laboratory in South Africa: Phase 1 launches GC-FID residual solvents, GC-FID terpenes, and HPLC-DAD cannabinoid potency; Phase 2 adds LC-MS/MS pesticides/mycotoxins and ICP-MS heavy metals. Edit blue cells on the Inputs, Test Mix, CAPEX, and Staffing sheets. Formula cells update automatically.
Sheet | Purpose
Inputs | Scenario selector, inflation, tax, funding, and operating assumptions
Test Mix | Services, pricing, variable costs, service mix by year
CAPEX & Depreciation | Phase 1/2 equipment spend and straight-line depreciation
Staffing & OPEX | Lean staffing plan and operating expense schedule
Revenue Model | Annual service-line revenue, variable costs, and gross margin
P&L | Income statement from revenue to net income
Cash Flow | Free cash flow, funding, and ending cash balance
Break-Even | Samples/day break-even and sensitivity logic
Dashboard | Investor summary KPIs and charts
Sources | Key external references and model-source notes
Formatting convention
Blue text = hardcoded editable assumptions. Green text = formulas pulling from other worksheets. Black text = calculations within the same sheet. Yellow fill = key assumptions requiring investor/management confirmation. Numbers are formatted so zeros show as dashes and negative values show in red parentheses.

Model caveats
This workbook is a financial planning model, not a formal quote. Equipment, salaries, rental, and accreditation costs are editable assumptions and should be replaced with supplier quotes, signed leases, HR benchmarks, SANAS/SAHPRA fee schedules, and tax advice before investor closing. Heavy metals are modeled as ICP-MS, not LC-MS. Pesticides and mycotoxins are modeled as LC-MS/MS. Phase 1 can operate as a revenue-generating core lab while Phase 2 contaminant testing is outsourced or added when volume justifies the capital expense.
