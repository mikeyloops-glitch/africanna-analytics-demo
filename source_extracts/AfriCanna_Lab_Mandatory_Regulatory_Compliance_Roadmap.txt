AfriCanna Analytics (Pty) Ltd
Mandatory Regulatory, Licensing and Accreditation Roadmap
Cannabis Analytical Testing Laboratory - South Africa
Prepared for investor, licensing and accreditation planning
Purpose: This document lists the mandatory and strongly recommended approvals, licences, systems and evidence needed to open and operate a cannabis analytical testing laboratory in South Africa. It is structured for a lean Phase 1 launch using GC-FID and HPLC-DAD, followed by Phase 2 expansion into LC-MS/MS and ICP-MS for full contaminant compliance testing.
Important note: This document is a business planning and compliance-readiness guide. It is not legal advice. Final licence scope, permit requirements and submission wording should be confirmed directly with SAHPRA, SANAS, the National Department of Health and a South African regulatory attorney or RA/QA consultant before submission.

3. SAHPRA Licence: Cannabis Testing / Contract Laboratory Activity
For a cannabis analytical testing lab, SAHPRA is the central licensing authority where the lab will handle cannabis, cannabis resin, cannabinoids or scheduled substances for medicinal, research or regulated commercial purposes. SAHPRA licence forms include a form for “Licence Application to Manufacture Import or Export Medicines Scheduled Substances including Contract Testing Laboratories” and a cannabis-specific form for cultivation/manufacture/import of cannabis for medicinal purposes.
What SAHPRA will expect
Practical evidence to prepare
Defined licensed activity
State exactly whether the site will test cannabis plant material, cannabis resin, extracts, cannabinoids, finished products and/or contract-test for licensed producers.
Site Master File / facility description
Facility layout, sample flow, controlled access zones, storage areas, instrument rooms, waste areas, safety controls and utilities.
Quality system
Quality manual, SOPs, document control, deviation/CAPA system, data integrity policy, change control, complaint handling and audit programme.
Personnel and responsible persons
CVs, qualifications, training records, organogram, job descriptions, technical manager, quality function and authorized signatories.
Security and controlled substance control
Access control, sample receipt, chain-of-custody, cannabis inventory, secure storage, reconciliation, destruction/disposal records.

Validated or verified methods for potency, residual solvents and terpenes in Phase 1; acceptance criteria and uncertainty approach.
Inspection readiness
Evidence that the facility and QMS are operational before inspection: logs, SOP implementation, training, equipment qualification, calibration.
3.1 Steps to Obtain the SAHPRA Licence
1. Confirm exact licence scope: testing only, contract testing, handling of cannabis resin/extracts, import/export of standards or samples, and whether medicinal cannabis clients are included.
2. Select and secure the premises. Do not design the lab generically: design it around controlled sample flow, restricted storage, instrument rooms, chemical storage, waste exit route and security.
3. Prepare the Site Master File and facility drawings. Include access control, utilities, HVAC/temperature control where relevant, storage areas and instrument locations.
4. Build the QMS skeleton before submission: quality manual, core SOPs, personnel responsibilities, sample chain-of-custody, data integrity, CAPA, document control and training system.
5. Prepare method package: method summaries, validation plan, equipment list, calibration plan and intended SANAS scope.
6. Complete the relevant SAHPRA form(s), pay the prescribed fee and submit supporting documentation.
7. Prepare for SAHPRA inspection. Run mock audits, check logs, prove controlled-substance records, prove that staff are trained on the SOPs.
8. Respond to findings/CAPAs. Close inspection observations with documented corrective action and evidence.
9. Receive licence, then operate only within the approved scope and conditions. Amend the licence if the facility, activities or controlled substance scope changes materially.
4. National Department of Health Section 22A Permit(s)
A cannabis lab may need a permit from the Director-General of Health where it acquires, uses, possesses, manufactures, supplies, imports or exports scheduled substances. This is especially important for THC-containing materials, reference standards and controlled cannabis samples. The exact permit type depends on final scope, product scheduling and whether the lab imports/exports standards or samples.
Permit need
When it applies
Evidence / action
Acquire/use/possess scheduled cannabis or THC material
When receiving, storing, preparing and analysing cannabis or cannabinoid samples that fall under scheduled substance controls.
Apply for Section 22A permit; provide facility, security, responsible person, SOPs and recordkeeping evidence.
Import/export controlled reference standards or samples
When importing certified reference materials, THC standards, cannabis samples, or exporting samples/results where controlled materials cross borders.
Check whether Section 22A(11) or import/export permit is needed; align with SAHPRA licence and customs documentation.
Disposal/destruction of controlled cannabis material
When destroying retained samples, extracts, standards or waste containing controlled cannabinoids.
Written destruction SOP, witness records, reconciliation, approved waste contractor where applicable.
5. SANAS ISO/IEC 17025 Accreditation
ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation is the technical proof that the laboratory is competent to produce valid, reliable and traceable testing results. SANAS accredits the laboratory for specific methods, matrices and analytes; it is not a generic “lab certificate”. A cannabis lab should pursue accreditation for each method that will appear on the Certificate of Analysis.
Accreditation scope item
Phase 1 target
Cannabinoid potency
HPLC-DAD/PDA method for flower, extracts and products as validated.
Terpene profile
GC-FID method using Rxi-624Sil MS or equivalent; validated analyte list.
Residual solvents
HS-GC-FID method using Rxi-624Sil MS or equivalent; validated solvent list.
Phase 2 pesticides/mycotoxins
LC-MS/MS method; scope extension after purchase, validation and proficiency evidence.
Phase 2 heavy metals
ICP-MS method; scope extension after digestion method and element panel validation.
5.1 What Must Exist Before Applying to SANAS
Quality manual aligned to ISO/IEC 17025:2017.
Defined impartiality and confidentiality controls.
Documented organisational structure and authority for technical and quality decisions.
SOPs for document control, records, sample handling, equipment, calibrations, method validation, data review, reporting, complaints, nonconforming work, CAPA, internal audit and management review.
Validated/verified methods with raw data and signed validation reports.
Measurement uncertainty budgets for quantitative methods.
Traceable calibration certificates for balances, pipettes, temperature devices, flow/pressure checks and reference standards where applicable.
Staff training matrix, competence records and authorisation records.
Internal QC plan: blanks, calibration checks, duplicates, spikes, CRMs, control charts and acceptance criteria.
Proficiency testing or inter-laboratory comparison plan. Where formal cannabis PT is unavailable, justify alternative comparison approaches.
Internal audit completed and management review completed before external assessment.
5.2 SANAS Accreditation Process
1. Define scope clearly: test method, matrix, analytes, range and uncertainty approach.
2. Purchase/access the current ISO/IEC 17025 standard and relevant SANAS requirement documents.
3. Build and implement the QMS; do not only write SOPs - create records proving use.
4. Validate methods and generate evidence of fitness for purpose.
5. Perform internal audit and management review. Correct findings before SANAS assessment.
6. Submit SANAS application with proposed scope and documentation.
7. Complete document review and pre-assessment/full assessment.
8. Close nonconformances with root cause, corrective action and evidence.
9. Receive accreditation certificate and schedule. Maintain through surveillance assessments and scope updates.

12. Certificate of Analysis and Result Sign-Off
Every report must be technically defensible. The sign-off workflow should be built into the QMS and LIMS from day one.
Analyst completes sample prep, sequence and first-level checks.
Batch QC must pass: blanks, calibration verification, duplicates/spikes and system suitability.
Technical reviewer checks chromatograms, integrations, calculations, QC and uncertainty where reported.
Authorized Signatory releases the Certificate of Analysis only within their approved scope of competence.
Reports must clearly state accredited vs non-accredited methods during pre-accreditation or when a method is outside the SANAS schedule.
All raw data, calculations, audit trails and report versions must be retained under document/record control.
