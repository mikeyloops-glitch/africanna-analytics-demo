Standardized Cannabis Flower Terpene Analysis
Core-18 analyte panel, sample preparation, vial loading, GC method options, and reporting calculations
Prepared for method-planning and documentation purposes | May 2026
1. Executive Summary
The analytical sample is the representative, homogenized cannabis flower itself, not the ethanol/solvent. Solvent or headspace preparation transfers volatile terpenes from the flower into an injectable phase or vapor phase for GC analysis.
The standard analytical platform is gas chromatography with MS or FID detection. HS-GC, HS-SPME-GC-MS, and solvent extraction followed by liquid-injection GC-MS/GC-FID are all common. ASTM WK87438 describes a headspace-GC procedure for Cannabis sativa plant materials and recognizes MS and FID detectors as suitable.
For a practical compliance-style panel, this document standardizes a core-18 terpene/terpenoid list. The list is derived from common cannabis lab panels and commercial cannabis terpene CRMs, especially Restek Cannabis Terpenes Standard #1, which contains 19 major cannabis-lab terpenes, plus Standard #2 for caryophyllene oxide and eucalyptol.
Results are normally reported as mg/g, micrograms/g, or % w/w. Total terpenes equals the sum of quantified target analytes, not every terpene present in the plant.
2. Standardized Core-18 Terpene Analyte Panel
There is no single globally mandatory “18-terpene” list. Laboratories define the validated panel based on the column, detector, reference standards, resolution, and jurisdictional/client requirements. The following core-18 is a practical standardized panel for cannabis flower and is intentionally aligned to analytes found in common cannabis terpene CRM mixes and COAs.

4. Analytical Method Options
4.1 Preferred instrument family
Terpenes are volatile/semi-volatile compounds, so GC is the standard separation technique. Detection is commonly by MS for confirmation/selected-ion quantitation or by FID for robust quantitation once identity is established. ASTM WK87438 describes HS-GC for Cannabis sativa plant materials and states the method is independent of detector type, with both MS and FID used in practice.
4.2 Headspace / HS-SPME-GC-MS workflow
Best for volatile monoterpenes and solvent-free profiling. A typical HS-SPME setup from MilliporeSigma uses 0.5 g dried cannabis in a 10 mL headspace vial, 30 min equilibration at 40 °C, 20 min headspace extraction at 40 °C, and 3 min fiber desorption at 270 °C on a GC column. The closed headspace vial minimizes loss of volatiles once sealed.
Parameter
Typical practice
Sample size
0.2–0.5 g homogenized dried flower is typical; 0.5 g in a 10 mL vial is a published HS-SPME example.
Vial
10 mL or 20 mL crimp/screw headspace vial with PTFE/silicone septum; seal immediately after weighing.
Equilibration
Typically 40 °C, 20–30 min. Higher temperatures increase response but can shift equilibrium and risk losses/artifacts.
Extraction/injection
Static headspace, HS-SPME fiber, HS-SPME Arrow, or headspace syringe. HS-SPME commonly uses DVB/CAR/PDMS fiber.
Detector
GC-MS for ID/confirmation; GC-FID for routine quantitation; both require calibration with reference standards.
4.3 Solvent extraction + liquid-injection GC-MS workflow
Best when the lab wants stronger recovery of less-volatile sesquiterpenes and oxygenated terpenoids. Restek’s validated LI-syringe-GC-MS workflow used 0.5 g homogenized flower/shake in a 10 mL ASE cell, IPA extraction, final extract volume of 12 mL, filtration through 0.22 µm PTFE, and 500 µL extract in a 2 mL autosampler vial with internal standard. Frontiers published a comparable preparation and also described a manual option: 0.5 g homogenized flower plus 12 mL IPA in a 50 mL centrifuge tube, vortex 3 min, sonicate 5 min at 40 °C, centrifuge 3 min, then analyze extract.
Step
Documentation-ready description
1. Homogenize
Break representative dried flower into a uniform sample. Minimize heat and open-air time to avoid terpene loss.
2. Weigh sample
Common method mass: 0.5 g. A 1.0 g sample can be used only if extraction volume, calibration range, and recovery are validated.
3A. ASE extraction
Place 0.5 g into 10 mL ASE cell, fill void with diatomaceous earth, extract with IPA; published conditions include 75 °C, 1500 psi, 5 min static, one cycle, 90 sec purge, final volume 12 mL.
3B. Manual extraction
Alternative: 0.5 g in 50 mL centrifuge tube + 12 mL IPA; vortex 3 min, sonicate at 40 °C for 5 min, centrifuge 3 min.
4. Filter
Filter aliquot through 0.22 µm PTFE into a clean vial.
5. Autosampler vial
Transfer a measured aliquot, e.g., 500 µL or 1 mL, into a 2 mL GC autosampler vial; add internal standard where used; cap immediately.
6. Analyze
Inject by split/splitless liquid injection into GC-MS or GC-FID. Quantify against multi-point calibration standards.
5. Sample Packaging Into the Vial
Use case
Recommended vial handling
Flower for HS/HS-SPME
Use a clean 10 or 20 mL headspace vial. Tare vial if needed, add 0.5 g homogenized dried flower, avoid compacting sample, immediately seal with PTFE/silicone septum cap. Record sample ID, mass, vial ID, date/time sealed, and analyst.
Calibration/QC for HS
Prepare standards/QCs in matched headspace vials. Some HS methods use salt/water matrices to force partitioning; one study prepared standards in 20 mL HS vials with NaCl, standard solution, and water.
Extract for liquid injection
Use an amber or clear 2 mL GC autosampler vial with low-bleed septum. Add filtered extract aliquot and internal standard; ensure sufficient headspace remains; cap tightly; avoid extended bench exposure.
Holding/handling
Keep samples sealed, cool, and protected from light. Avoid repeated opening; terpene loss is a real pre-analytical variable.
6. Calibration, Quantitation, and Reporting Units
Quantitation is not based on smelling the vial or visually reading a chromatogram. Each terpene is quantified against certified reference standards and a calibration curve. Internal standards, such as deuterated naphthalene in the Restek workflow, can correct for injection variability and improve method control.
For solvent extraction, use the following calculation:
Terpene (µg/g) = [Cextract (µg/mL) × final extract volume (mL) × dilution factor] ÷ sample mass (g)
Unit
Meaning
µg/g
Primary mass-normalized result; common in method development papers.
mg/g
µg/g ÷ 1000. Often easier for COAs.
% w/w
mg/g ÷ 10. Example: 10 mg/g = 1.0% w/w.
Total terpenes
Sum of target analytes quantified above LOQ; not the full universe of cannabis terpenes.
Example: 0.5 g flower extracted to 12 mL. If β-myrcene in the extract is 50 µg/mL and no additional dilution is made: (50 × 12) ÷ 0.5 = 1,200 µg/g = 1.2 mg/g = 0.12% w/w.
7. Can Terpenes and Cannabinoids Be Analyzed on the Same Column?
Technically, yes, certain GC liquid-injection methods can monitor terpenes and cannabinoids in the same chromatographic run/column, but this is not the usual best-practice compliance setup. Terpenes are volatile and elute early; cannabinoids are much less volatile, higher-boiling compounds and may require derivatization or high-temperature GC conditions. A Waters application note specifically states that solvent extraction and liquid injection allow monitoring cannabidiol and THC in the same method, while standard headspace does not recover THC/CBD well because cannabinoids are not sufficiently volatile.
Practical recommendation: validate terpenes and cannabinoids as separate methods unless there is a strong operational reason to combine them. A combined GC method can be useful for R&D screening, but separate terpene GC and cannabinoid HPLC methods are usually cleaner for regulated potency reporting.
8. Suggested Documentation Template for a Flower Terpene SOP
Scope: Quantitative analysis of core-18 terpenes/terpenoids in dried cannabis flower.
Matrix: Representative dried flower, homogenized immediately before analysis.
Sample mass: 0.5 g validated default; 1.0 g acceptable only after method verification.
Preparation route A: HS/HS-SPME for solvent-free volatile terpene profiling.
Preparation route B: IPA solvent extraction + liquid-injection GC-MS/FID for broader monoterpene/sesquiterpene quantitation.
Calibration: Multi-point calibration using CRMs, preferably matrix-matched or surrogate-matrix matched where possible.
QC: Method blank, continuing calibration verification, laboratory control sample/spike, duplicate sample, and internal standard response checks.
Acceptance: Retention time match, qualifier ion confirmation for MS methods, calibration fit acceptance, blank control, and precision/recovery criteria defined during validation.
9. Key References Used
ASTM International. WK87438: New Test Methods for Analysis of Terpenoids in Cannabis Materials using Head-Space Gas Chromatography (HS-GC). Describes HS-GC procedures for Cannabis sativa plant materials and recognizes MS/FID detection.
Restek. “Analysis of Terpenes in Cannabis Flower via LI-Syringe-GC-MS” (2023). Provides validated LI-syringe-GC-MS workflow, 0.5 g sample mass, 12 mL final extract volume, filtration, internal standard addition, calibration range, precision, recovery, and example terpene results.
Myers C. et al. “Accelerated Solvent Extraction of Terpenes in Cannabis Coupled With Various Injection Techniques for GC-MS Analysis.” Frontiers in Chemistry, 2021. Compares ASE and manual solvent extraction; describes 0.5 g flower, IPA extraction, and vial preparation approaches.
MilliporeSigma / Supelco. “GC Analysis of Terpenes in Cannabis on Equity-1 after Headspace SPME.” Provides example HS-SPME conditions: 0.5 g dried cannabis in 10 mL headspace vial, 40 °C equilibration/extraction, DVB/CAR/PDMS fiber, 60 m GC column.
Waters. “Terpenes in Hemp and Cannabis Determined Using EI GC-MS/MS.” Notes that solvent extraction/liquid injection can monitor CBD/THC in the same method, while standard headspace is unsuitable for THC/CBD recovery because cannabinoids are not sufficiently volatile.
Restek. “Cannabis Certified Reference Materials for Cannabis Analysis.” Lists Cannabis Terpenes Standard #1, 19 components, formulated to contain major terpenes analyzed by cannabis labs, plus Standard #2 for caryophyllene oxide and eucalyptol.
